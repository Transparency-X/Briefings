#### 2. Applicable Legal & Policy Framework
*We reference these frameworks to ground our discussion in current statutory obligations:*

-   **Criminal Justice (Hate Offences) Act 2024:** Establishes aggravated offences for hate motivation; requires AGS to record, investigate, and present hate motivation as a distinct evidentiary element separate from the underlying offence.
-   **Domestic Violence Act 2018 (as amended):** Criminalises coercive control; extends protections to non-spousal intimate partners and persons in “close relationships.” Directly applicable to immigration-based coercion where residency status is weaponised.
-   **EU Victims’ Rights Directive (2012/29/EU):** Mandates individual risk assessments, right to information, and protection from secondary victimisation for all victims, irrespective of residence or citizenship status.
-   **International Protection Act 2015 & SI 434/2023:** Provides for independent immigration legal advice for victims of abuse/hate crime; establishes emergency residency permissions for those whose status is contingent on an abusive sponsor.
-   **AGS Victim Service Standards (2023):** Commits to 7-day initial contact, named liaison officer, and culturally competent service delivery for vulnerable victims.
-   **Common Travel Area (CTA) & Post-Brexit Arrangements:** Governs cross-border movement and rights recognition between Ireland, Northern Ireland, and Great Britain. Relevant for victims who have fled or been coerced across jurisdictions.

#### 3. Documented Operational Gaps & Community Concerns
*Based on community reporting, NGO case data, and victim testimonies:*

| Issue Area | Documented Gap | Operational Impact |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Post-Reporting Safeguards | Risk assessments frequently omit co-targeted family/friends, digital threat vectors, and immigration vulnerability. Timelines for first substantive update exceed 7-day standard. | Victims remain unsafe during critical early investigation period; under-reporting due to perceived futility. |
| Evidence Admissibility | No standardised guidance for digital evidence (metadata, chain of custody). Hate motivation under-documented when underlying offence appears “minor” (trespass, harassment). | Cases fail at prosecution stage; perpetrators escalate due to perceived impunity. |
| Witness Intimidation | Retaliatory immigration complaints and threats against witnesses not consistently treated as new criminal offences or triggers for enhanced protection. | Witnesses withdraw cooperation; cases collapse mid-investigation. |
| Immigration Weaponisation | Perpetrators routinely threaten deportation or withdrawal of sponsorship. No publicly available AGS protocol guaranteeing investigation-immigration firewall. | Victims avoid reporting entirely; abusers exploit state systems as tools of control. |
| Forced Return / Cross-Border | Naturalised citizens and Irish-born LGBTQI+ individuals coerced into returning to persecutory jurisdictions lack tailored risk assessments. Victims who flee to EU/UK face barriers to remote testimony and continued investigation. | Jurisdictional gaps enable transnational abuse; victims re-traumatised by system failure. |
| Intersectional Vulnerability | Mixed-status families, undocumented individuals, and neurodivergent/disabled LGBTQI+ victims face compounded barriers with no tailored protocols. | Systemic exclusion of most vulnerable cohorts from justice pathways. |
